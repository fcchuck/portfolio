EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Mr. Thomas, owner and CEO of Thomas’ House of Antiques & Treasures (THAT) has contracted International power to develop an Information System (THATIS) to increase profits for the auction house.  The assumptions and requirements are set out in the System Request.  The proposed THATIS meets all these requirements.

Based on the Cost Benefit Analysis THAT will break even at the end of the first year after implementing THATIS.  This is based on the expected reduction in costs as well as increase in revenues that the automated system will help generate.  THATIS will result in approximately $900,000 added to the bottom line as a result of increased revenue and decreased expenses.

The planning for the project took 54 days as planning started on 2/4/2014 and concluded on 4/8/2014.  The team, International Power, consists of 5 members who have been with the project from its inception.

All functional and non-functional requirements are met resulting is a user-friendly multi-functional system with the following main characteristics:

·         Interested customers can use the new website to check on the calendar of auctions, browse items for sale in the next auction and to register to participate in the auction;

·         The bidders’ database will be used to send electronic notifications of upcoming auctions and thus realize the expected savings in postage and printing costs;

·         Bidders can place bids in advance and during the auction;

·         Winning bidders’ invoices are prepared automatically, when payment is received the items are released with an option to hold, a fee applies after 7 days;

·         Consignors’ merchandise is entered into inventory and assigned to lots, items are removed from inventory as they are sold.  If unsold they are placed in inventory for the next auction;

·         Financial reports are generated based on information garnered from transactions recorded in the system.

Based on preliminary results from the use-case, class, sequence and state machine diagrams the system, as designed, provides the requested requirements. 

The usability test provided important feedback and potential user approval.  In spite of the positive feedback we recommend further enhancements to include customer feed-back (as customer service was a concern), links to payment services such as pay-pal and the possibility of a payroll system tied into the time clock.

If this project is to proceed then we must start planning the actual implementation, to include purchasing the hardware and software.  As this is taking place International Power will start planning the transition to include installation and training of the staff.

PROJECT/CASE SCENARIO

 

Thomas’ House of Antiques & Treasures (THAT) is one of the local community’s most exciting Thursday night events.   Established in 1992, THAT auctions people’s prized heirlooms and estate treasures.  Every Thursday night from 6:00 p.m. until the last item has been “knocked-down,” Coyote Clem, THAT’s colorful auctioneer, urges the audience to bid ever so higher in order to maximize the selling price of the item up for bid.  The item caller, and also owner, Thomas, is ever so mindful that to garner the best price for consignor’s items, he needs to embellish the description of the item, often referring to its antiquity, design, rarity, and the possible missed opportunity of a life time.  As an astute business man, Thomas knows that he must sell at least $10,000 worth of merchandise per hour, so being organized and knowing that the items being auctioned have reached their greatest potential are critical in achieving this goal. 

All auctions are preceded with previews that allow potential bidders the chance to examine the items closely.  Bidders register to bid at each auction by filling out a registration form that includes the bidder’s name, address, contact information, business affiliation if any, tax number, and other demographic/business information.  A number is assigned to a bidder upon registering and this number is used to record and track winning bids for the items auctioned.  There are “preferred bidders” who are identified as those who repeatedly attend the auctions and purchase items frequently at the auctions.  These preferred customers are assigned a permanent number that is kept on file in the office, enabling these bidders the privilege of not having to register each time an auction is held. 

Some customers register for a bidder number and attend the preview that begins the day of the auction, but not the actual auction.  These ‘absentee’ bidders leave a card with the item number, brief description, and the highest bid amount they are willing to bid for the item.  The customer may win the bid with a figure that is lower than the amount submitted if there is little interest and slow bidding for the item. 

There are other customers who register and do not attend the auction, but telephone-in their bids as the items they are interested in are put up for auction.  These customers may have a scheduling conflict and cannot physically attend the auction, but this allows them the opportunity to participate.  All registered bidders for each auction are kept on file, even if they do not receive a winning bid.  These bidders/customers’ names and addresses are used for special auction brochure mailings and phone/email advertising of up-coming auctions.  Those without permanent numbers must register for each auction and will receive a unique bidding number each time.

There are those customers who consign items for auction.  These customers are called clients.  Some of these clients consign frequently, but most of the consigned goods result from estates that are being liquidated as a result of a family wishing to settle a will, or from elderly people who are moving to a smaller house or apartment and cannot take all their family heirlooms with them.  In many cases, Thomas will purchase estates to lessen the trauma for the family.  This reduces the complexity of inventorying the items and frequently results in Thomas generating jobs and more income for his workers and the business.  To provide customers with an eclectic mixture of fine antiques and special-interest items each week, Thomas relies not only on consignments of estates, but also contracts with vendors of oriental carpets, French porcelains, silver, fine paintings, crystal, and garden statuary, who want to reduce their inventory.    Maintaining accurate data that enables him to manage his transactions with these vendors is essential to a successful business relationship.

Whenever a bidder decides to leave, he or she settles the account with the checkout staff located at the registration counter of the auction room.  The checkout person uses the bidder’s number to determine the successful bids and the total amount owed, including tax.  The final invoice includes the winning bids amount, a buyer’s premium (10% if payment is made in cash or by check, 13% if a credit card is used), and sales tax (5% of the total amount).  If the bidder has a business license, this number is recorded and no sales tax is collected.

Thomas employs four office staff and an auctioneer full-time, and keeps a list of temporary employees who help with running the auction by displaying the items for bidders to see what is being auctioned, and then assisting in removing the items to a storage area where customers can pick them up after settling their account.  The receipt given to customers following the settlement of their account lists all the item numbers, the amount of the bid for each item, the total buyer’s premium amount, sales tax, the total amount owed, and the total amount paid.  This receipt is used as a control mechanism by the staff as they locate and assist the customers in loading the items into their vehicle.

One of the major tasks during the days prior to each auction is inventorying all the items to be auctioned.  Numbers are assigned to each item using a code to determine the consignor and the specific item.  These item numbers are written on a label that is affixed to the auction item and this number is recorded on a master list with an appropriate description for each item so there is no mistake as to the identification of the actual item.  Photos of the items are taken and stored with the item description.  Two copies of the master list are made; one is provided to the client/consignor, and the other is kept on file for reconciliation after the auction.  Within a week following the auction, the reconciled list, with the winning bid amounts, is used to reimburse the client for the sale of the consigned goods.  A seller’s commission of 30% of the total sales is charged the client for all services provided in conducting the auction. 

Thomas has always wanted to find an easier and more accurate way of tagging all the items with coded numbers.  The current manual process is time consuming and expensive, and in some cases has resulted in the replication of numbers on different consigned items.  Reconciling the auction results in these cases created problems with both customers/bidders, and clients/consignors.  Items are sometimes lost and are not accounted for, thus causing an air of distrust by customers and clients.  Thomas wants to ensure those who do business with THAT have positive experience and will return for repeat business.  

Thomas relies on the data collected on the auction sales to help in understanding the market.  Reports are generated that serve to identify what items are selling best, and those which are slow movers, and any trends in customer or client participation.  Management reports, detailing weekly, monthly, quarterly, and yearly sales are produced for financial management purposes.  

Currently, THAT uses a couple of PCs to record bidder information for auction brochure mailings.  Electronic spreadsheet software is used to create simple financial reports.  However, there is very little strategic use of customer and client data for providing a competitive advantage over other auction houses in the surrounding area.  A website has been created that lists the items for the up-coming auctions.  This data is keyed by hand and only provides the potential bidder short descriptions of the items.  It sometimes contains errors which upset bidders.  Thomas would like to be able to show bidders pictures of the items in hopes of generating more interest.  The website listing is usually not completed until just prior to the auction, so very little value has resulted from this labor-intensive effort.

Thomas is somewhat computer literate, but the staff does not have the required skills to design and build an information system to help streamline the business processes at THAT.  Everyone recognizes the need for more accurate data and useful information.  They know the business but not the information technology, and they don't have the systems development skills to implement a good computer-based solution. Thomas would like to present his executive board a proposal for developing such a system for approval and begin the project as soon as possible.

INFORMATION SYSTEM BUSINESS CASE

 

 

 

Project Scope

 

The requested Information System Thomas’ House of Antiques & Treasures (THATIS) will provide the opportunity for the business association to increase revenue, track and control expenses and improve customer satisfaction, for both bidders and seller.

The new system will automate the checkout and invoicing process for bidders while allowing THAT to automate the auction registration process so as to maintain customer (bidder, vendor, consignors) data as well as manage and track auction sales information.  In sum, the THATIS will provide a smoother and more enjoyable experience to the bidders which will result in an increase in customer base while also making the selling process easier for selling customers while decreasing lost and misplaced items.  Internal operations for THAT will be much more efficient and cost effective.

THATIS will automate auction item inventory data collection and management of the inventory which will reduce loss and make payments to sellers faster and more accurate.

Internal operations at THAT will be improved by automated management of staff information, accurate and timely business reports, auction scheduling and improved website with accurate, real-time inventory, schedule, and promotion information as well as improved marketing through timely auction announcement supported by traditional brochure mailing.

ACTORS/STAKEHOLDERS:

  • Contact – any person who either visits the auction house in person or visits the website to either look at the merchandise offered or to receive information.  This person can become a bidder, if not they will not have further association with the process.
  • Bidder – A person with active participation in the auction by having registered and made an offer for merchandise.  This can be either in person through direct bidding or having visited prior to the auction and left an “absentee bid” or having left an “absentee bid” on the website.  Online bidding during the actual auction is not possible at this time, but might be a later upgrade.  The bidder interacts with the following use cases:  Registration, Bidding, Paying and Pick up of Merchandise.
  • Preferred Bidder - A specialized actor as he inherits the behavior of the bidder but interacts with the process in a different way as he may skip the registration process and have special payment arrangements.
  • Consignor -  Also referred to as clients, consign items for auction with THAT.  Receives payments  from THAT for items sold, minus the 30% commission which is THAT’s revenue.  This actor interacts with Consign Merchandise and Pay Consignor use cases.
  • Auctioneer – The person in charge of running the auction event.  He must be able to assess interest in an item, start with a reasonable price and drive it up through bids while keeping track of “absentee bids”.  Must also be able to gauge interest and mood of bidders to determine items to bring up.  Interacts with Place in inventory and Offer Items use cases as he must be able to call-up items from inventory based on his assessment of the bidders.
  • Inventory Manager – Employed by THAT to receive, inventory and store items brought in by consignors or purchased directly by Thomas.  Assigns workers to move items from inventory to the auction block as well as moving purchased items to the check out area for bidders to pick up after paying for them.  Interacts with Place in Inventory, primarily but has an effect on Offer Item and Pick Up Item.
  • THAT staff – The full time staff employed by the auction house to assist with inventory, provide the purchase items to the bidders and assist in any manner needed for the smooth functioning of the auction.  Interact with Place in Inventory and final interactions with Bidders.
  • Workers – Part-time staff brought in on auction nights to supplement THAT workers.  Perform non-financial tasks depending on needs of the auction.  These are specialized actors linked to THAT workers as they perform some of the same tasks and have some of the same interactions.
  • Business Manager – Employed by THAT with two primary responsibilities, first  to monitor payments received from bidders and payments to consignors and second to complete financial reports for Thomas regarding the operation of the auction house.  As a secondary responsibility he is in charge of payment of staff and workers unless this function gets contracted out.  Interacts with the Figure Final Price and Pay consignor use cases.
  • Owner – Mr. Thomas himself.  As CEO of the company he is responsible for everything his staff  do or fail to do.  More than interactions with use cases he is more involved in interactions with actors themselves such as supervising and directing their activities.  Thomas is directly involved in purchasing items that will be auctioned as well as negotiating with consignors and then making sure consignors are paid correctly for items sold, as well as currently items lost due to poor inventory practices.

 

 

USE CASE DESCRIPTION AND FLOW OF EVENTS

  • Automated Registration:  Bidders register for the auction.   The actors are new bidders, preferred bidders and contacts, who will become bidders once they complete the registration.  Steps:
    1. Log onto website or be physically present at auction house;
    2. Contacts and new bidders enter requested information;
    3. Preferred bidders enter their password;
    4. View information on items on inventory available for bidding.
  • Bidding:  Bidders make their bid.  Actors are bidders, encouraged by auctioneer.  Steps:
    1. Bidders place “absentee bids” either prior to auction/item being placed on auction block either online or during viewing of merchandise;
    2. Auctioneer places initial price on item, uses “absentee bids” (based on guidelines) when they exist;
    3. Bidders present during auction place their bids;
    4. Auctioneer takes bids, announces winner when no more bids.
  • Automated Checkout:
    1. Once Item is won the Bidders number and item number are associated;
  • Automated Invoice (Figure Final Price):  Winning bidder is charged correct amount.  Actors are bidders THAT staff and Business Manager.  Steps:
    1. Staff enter winning bidder number and item won in person or through “absentee bid’ in system;
    2. Winning bidder is presented a bill that includes correct buyer premium based on method of payment plus tax if applicable;
    3. Bidder will pay online, with smartphone or at cashier
  • Update Consignor AP (Pay Consignor):  Clients who provided merchandise to sell will receive payment for items sold at auction.  Actors are the consignors, the business office manager and the owner.  Steps:
    1. System verifies items sold for each consignor;
    2. System deducts auctions house commission, 30%;
    3. System cuts checks;
    4. Business Manager verifies and signs checks;
    5. Owner signs checks and presents checks to consignors.
  • Manage Business Reports:
  • System will receive updated information on revenue from purchases;
  • System will receive information on payments to consignors;
  • Reports will be generated showing Transactions and results;
  • Financial data will be queried as needed.
  • Update Bidder Database (Maintain Bidder Information):  Bidder information automatically entered in database upon registration.  Actors are bidders as IS system does the rest.  Steps:
    1. Bidders and contacts complete registration online;
    2. Bidders and contacts at auction house  complete paper registration;
    3. Information saved in database.
  • Manage Website:
    1. Auction Schedules will be available for viewing;
    2. Online registration will be available;
    3. Online bidding will be available;
    4. Online payment will be available.
  • Online Mailings:  Automatic marketing material sent to bidders in the database.  Actors are auction house staff who maintain the system and decrease physical mailing and printing as online material replaces it.  Steps:
    1. System accesses bidders contact data;
    2. Announcements and brochures are e-mailed to bidders instead of using postal service’
  • Item Collection (Add to Inventory):  Consignors bring merchandise to consign as well as the owner purchases estates and other merchandise, all of which is placed in inventory.  Actors are consignors, the owner, the Inventory manager and staff who enter items and pull them as needed to auction them.  Steps:
    1. Consignors and owners bring merchandise;
    2. Inventory manager adds to inventory;
    3. Staff tags the items;
    4. Auctioneer system is updated with new items;
    5. Staff places items in warehouse.
  • Manage Inventory:
    1. System will track items collected and tagged;
    2. System will update Auctioneer as to items available vs. sold.
  • Manage Auctioneer System (Offer item):  During auction items are brought from warehouse to floor to be sold.  Actor is the auctioneer who can use his system to request items as he feels will be sold.  Steps:
    1. Auctioneer’s system has current inventory;
    2. Auctioneer calls up items either individually or by lots;
    3. Items offered for sale; system updated as items are sold with price and buyer number;
    4. Auctioneer calls up next item.

STATE MACHINE DIAGRAM

 

 

State Machine Diagram Descriptions

1)      Item:  Instantiation of AuctionInventoryItem class.  Represents one individual item presented by consignor to auction house for auctioning.

2)      In_Inventory:  Accepted by THAT and in this state having attributes of AuctionInventoryItem.

3)      In-Auction_Lot:  Item waiting to be auctioned when Auction_Lot meets sufficient criteria [items=<10].

4)      In-Auction: State changes to In-Auction when the item is scheduled to be put up for for bid.

5)      Sold:  A bid amount which is equal to or greater than reserve bid.

6)      Paid: Successful bidder pays for item and checks up.

7)      Paid_Stored:  Bidder pays for item and schedules future pick-up.

8)      Picked-Up:  Bidder presents invoice/receipts and takes possession of the item.

9)      Archived:  Final state for Item after passing through states In-Auction_Lot, In-Auction, Sold, Paid for and Picked-Up.  Item is physically out of auction house and is now an entry into records.

10)  Returned_to_Consignor:  If Item did not equal or exceed reserve bid amount during three different auctions or Item was damaged beyond repair while in-Inventory.

11)  Damaged:  Item damaged during transporting from the warehouse to the auction.

12)  Donated: An Item not accepted by the consignor as a return due to damage while in In_Inventory state and not able to be repaired.

USABILITY TESTING

Once the team members of International Power designed, created and tested the THAT website another step was taken.  The website was presented to two non-team members with different backgrounds and experience who would represent a cross-section of users.  The two testers were:

v  Michelle A. Kelo, an Information Systems Architect with Capital One who holds a Master’s degree in Computer Science with a strong background in computer applications and programming.  Currently Michelle is working on several projects related to user access and utilization of banking websites and services.

v  Savannah A. Nelson, a junior marketing major at VCU with several years of experience in retail sales and management who is very familiar with computer use for many activities of daily living.

Both testers were presented with the finished product separately and asked to access all pages and use all functions available (a temporary user-name and password was provided to each tester).  Based on feedback from our testers we made several upgrades to the original product:

1.      Original colors were toned down to make more pleasing to the eye.  Both had agreed that the original bright colors were interesting at first sight but became distracting and detrimental.  Both also agreed that the current color scheme worked well for the staff pages, as these pages are functional and will be accessed repeatedly, but were also pleasing to the eye in the public areas.  Color scheme should remain constant so as to remind staff they are still on THAT’s website.

2.      Both testing users agreed that the flow of the site, order in which pages were accessed as well as order of links to other pages, was logical and easy to follow. 

3.      Michelle suggested a change in the page layout for the Home page from the original layout.  This change involved moving the bullet items higher on the page and placing the encouragement to explore further lower on the page.  The changes were made.  Savannah did not see a difference in either layout.

4.      Savannah, who is online for school as well as accessing both social and entertainment sites for at least 6 hours a day, felt that the website was functional and business-like but lacked many of the characteristics expected from current sites.  She agreed with Michelle on the good functionality of the staff pages but admitted that the public pages were designed for an “older crowd”, which is consistent with the demographics observed during live auctions.  Michelle, who fits into the demographics of a population willing and able to purchase merchandise offered by THAT, liked the public pages.

5.      When asked what the ideal website would have that THAT’s site is lacking Savannah immediately mentioned more interactive pages where each item for sale is shown in more detail with information displayed.  Michelle liked that idea but understands the time and labor constraints and felt that this site was appropriate for an auction house.  Both agreed that an app for a smart phone would be an asset as well as automatic reminders of upcoming auctions.  Savannah did inquire about staff having the capability to access their work schedules on the site.
